Iraq Market Brief, Q1 2026

How a global supply chain shock reshaped Iraq's market in Q1 2026.

A January customs overhaul, a March closure of the Strait of Hormuz, and a near-halving of oil output collided in the first quarter. The result, observed across IIACSS retail audits and consumer fieldwork: imports slowed, essential prices climbed 25–50%, and household buying behavior shifted measurably. This brief sets out what we observed, what the data shows, and what it means for clients operating in the market.

IIACSS Research Group · Q1 2026 Retail Audit & Fieldwork · Published April 2026 · 5 min read

Key findings

  • 01Supply, not demand, is driving price movement. Imports from Iran and Kuwait have slowed materially. Higher tariffs under Cabinet Decision No. 957 plus the Hormuz closure created a supply squeeze now visible at shelf level.
  • 02Essential dry goods up 25–50%; tobacco up 15–40%. Locally produced soft drinks, snacks, and several FMCG categories remain stable. Imported goods rose where still available.
  • 03Local brands are gaining shelf share, especially in dairy. Substitution is partial, local capacity cannot fully offset reduced imports in the short term.
  • 04Consumer channel shift to wholesale and stockpiling of staples. Households are not buying more; they are buying more carefully, prioritizing essentials.
  • 05Regional variation is significant. Basra and the south are absorbing the strongest pressure on essentials and retailer margins. The north is faring better.

01 · SupplyThe shock is supply-side, not demand-side

The pattern observed in Q1 is unusual. Prices are rising and certain SKUs (A Stock Keeping Unit) are disappearing from shelves, but consumer demand has not increased, IIACSS retail audits show foot traffic and basket sizes flat-to-down across the quarter. The market is receiving less, not buying more.

Three drivers compound: tariffs raised from a 1–5% range to as high as 30% under Cabinet Decision No. 957 (effective January 1); a $5,000 quality certification fee on imported goods; and the late-February closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which interrupted shipping into Iraq's southern ports. Imports from Iran and Kuwait, historically two of Iraq's largest sources for FMCG and dry goods, slowed materially through February and March.

02 · Local productionLocal brands are gaining share , but cannot fully fill the gap

IIACSS shelf audits show local brands gaining placement in dairy and adjacent categories as imports thin out. The substitution is real but partial: local manufacturing capacity is absorbing some of the gap, not all of it.

Implications for clients

Local manufacturers have a window to lock in shelf space and distribution that may be hard to displace once imports normalize. Trade marketing investment in Q2 will compound.

Importers and multinationals should expect continued shelf-space erosion in dairy, basic FMCG, and any SKU dependent on Iran/Kuwait routing. Alternative sourcing through Jordan and Turkey is operational but at materially higher landed cost.

03 · ConsumersChannel shift toward wholesale; stockpiling of essentials

IIACSS retail audits across Q1 show a clear behavioral shift. Households are moving more spending toward wholesale and semi-wholesale outlets , driven by both price and availability. Stockpiling is concentrated in rice, oil, sugar, and legumes. Non-essential categories show mixed behavior: snack consumption continues to spike in social-media-driven cycles even under broader pressure.

You can see movement is less than before. People are using money only to buy the necessary food items. They are afraid of spending money on other things.
, Focus group participant, Baghdad · IIACSS qualitative fieldwork, March 2026
Implications for clients

Brands in essentials should expect higher pack-size sensitivity and bulk preference. Wholesale channel investment is more efficient than modern trade promotion in this environment.

Brands in non-essentials should not assume blanket demand compression. Snacks and socially-driven categories remain buoyant; messaging and social-media-led activation continue to convert.

05 · RegionalPressure is not distributed evenly

IIACSS retail audits show the supply shock landing differently across geographies. In the north, shortages are visible, but price increases have been slower, cross-border supply lines provided some buffer. In the Euphrates region, the impact was more immediate, driven by border disruptions affecting trade from Syria and Jordan. In Basra and the south, pressure is strongest on essentials; audit data shows prices rising sharply and retailer margins tightening as costs outpace returns.

Implications for clients

National pricing strategies are losing accuracy. Regional pricing or trade-spend differentiation, particularly for the Basra/south corridor, should be reviewed against current audit data.

Retailer margin compression in the south is a real risk to listings and shelf execution. Manufacturers should expect harder negotiations and more requests for trade support in that region.

06 · OutlookWhat we expect through Q2

Imports remain constrained and stock levels are gradually declining. We expect price pressure on essentials to continue through Q2, even with the late-April easing in Hormuz access. The trajectory depends on three variables: the durability of the regional ceasefire, the pace of any tariff revision, and the speed at which local production scales into the gap.

Beyond Q2, the more important shift is structural. Trade flows are diversifying away from Iran/Kuwait routing toward Jordan and Turkey. Local production share is rising. Consumer channel preferences are migrating toward wholesale. These are likely durable changes, not temporary distortions.

What this means for planning

Current market conditions should not be treated as temporary. Companies may need to reassess sourcing, pricing, and channel strategies that were built around pre-2026 assumptions.

Track local-vs-imported share quarterly. The competitive landscape on shelf is moving faster than aggregate market data will reflect.

Treat sentiment data with caution. The 53% "right direction" reading reflects relief from the Hormuz crisis, not confidence in market conditions. The two move independently.

07 · TimelineFrom customs shock to cautious easing

Eight inflection points that shaped Iraq's market through Q1 2026 and into early Q2.

January 1, 2026
Customs overhaul takes effect

Cabinet Decision No. 957 raises tariffs from 1–5% to as high as 30%, alongside a new $5,000 quality certification fee on imported goods.

Trigger event
February 8, 2026
Budget pressure builds

The national budget , based on $63/barrel oil , faces a growing deficit estimated at $13.5 billion. Liquidity pressure compounds.

February 28, 2026
Regional escalation; Hormuz closed

Escalation between the US, Israel, and Iran pushes Brent crude toward $120/barrel. Iran closes the Strait of Hormuz, throwing southern shipping into uncertainty.

Critical disruption
March 4–12, 2026
Oil output halves

Iraqi oil exports collapse from 4.1 million to under 2 million barrels per day , heavy daily revenue losses for an oil-dependent economy.

Mid-March 2026
Household-level price shock

Some vegetables triple in price. Gas shortages produce long queues. Traders begin rerouting imports through Jordan and Turkey at materially higher cost.

Early April 2026
Budget vacuum at the federal level

Q2 begins without an approved national budget. Provinces respond by slowing or freezing infrastructure projects, deepening the slowdown.

April 9–23, 2026
Stock exchange holds

The Iraq Stock Exchange shows resilience , its main index up roughly 8% YTD. Banking activity continues; capital increases proceed.

Counter-signal
Late April 2026
Cautious easing

Ceasefire allows Hormuz shipping to resume. Oil eases. New harvests soften some food prices. The Central Bank reports dollar demand met; the dinar holds at ~1,307 per USD.
